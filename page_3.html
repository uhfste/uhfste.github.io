Worming(deworming)

When it comes to using chemical dewormers:

1. Employ deworming only when necessary, and avoid excessive usage. The most effective way to determine the right time for chemical deworming is through fecal testing. If you opt not to conduct tests, consider deworming every two months or more frequently if required.

2. Whenever you introduce a new sheep to your farm, deworm them on the day of their arrival. This precaution is essential because you have no knowledge of the care they received before coming to your farm.

3. Always deworm a ewe the day after she gives birth (kidding). This practice is crucial due to the increased vulnerability of ewes to worms caused by the stress of kidding, which depletes their resources. Additionally, the hormones released during kidding can activate dormant parasites that may exist in her system.

4. Keep an eye out for the presence of "rice-like" particles in the sheep's feces, as these could indicate the presence of tapeworms.

5. If you notice that a sheep appears thinner than expected.

Symptoms indicating a worm infestation in ovines include:

- Pale or white gums
- A lackluster coat
- Diarrhea
- Reduced milk production
- Stool consistency becoming clumpy
- Loss of appetite
- Fluffed or unkempt hair
- Lethargy
- Slightly elevated or subnormal body temperature
- Rumen failure
- Dehydration
- Persistent coughing, which may indicate lung worm infestation, characterized by a dry cough, especially after physical activity.
- The development of "bottle jaw," a swelling under the jaw, in severe infestations.
